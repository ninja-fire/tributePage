VISION

A global fashion industry that conserves and restores the environment and values people over growth and profit

WHY

women who make your clothes

In 2013, a building in the capital of Bangladesh, The Rana Plaza in Dhaka, collapsed on textile design workshops, causing several thousand injuries and around 1,135 deaths. The causes are simple: the eight-story building was in dire need of renovation, but the contractors (brands) and managers on site did not take into account the severity of the cracks and fragility of the building. In the rubble, labels of the brands Carrefour, Auchan, Camaïeu were found and Mango, Benetton or even Primark were known to place orders with these manufacturers. This story, which has caused a lot of noise and which is now a benchmark event in fashion, has above all made it possible to highlight practices known as “fast fashion” causing serious ecological and social damage.

HOW

Following this Rana Plaza incident, far from an isolated case, Carry Somers and Orsola de Castro, two British designers, decided to create the Fashion Revolution. The aim is to create a movement of action for the fashion industry by making it more transparent and less harmful to the environment and to textile workers. The Fashion Revolution is present in around 100 countries and you can follow the actions implemented in your country on social networks (one Instagram account exists per country). Fashion Revolution day, celebrated on April 24, marks the anniversary of the Rana Plaza event and brought out the hashtag #whomademyclothes, which was first featured on twitter in 2015 when it launched. Fashion Revolution Day was transformed in 2016 into Fashion Revolution Week which always takes place in April in 90 countries and brings together tens of thousands of people around the issue of fast fashion.

ACT

Your voice can change everything. Since Fashion Revolution started, people from all over the world have used their voice and their power to tell brands that things must change. And it’s working. The industry is starting to change. More brands are being open about where their clothes are made. More manufacturers are making their factories safer. More producers are being seen and heard. But the story is far from over. We are only just getting started. We can’t stop until every garment worker who makes the clothes we love is seen, heard, paid properly and working in safe conditions. Your voice does make a difference. We need to make this Fashion Revolution Week bigger and bolder than ever before. Ask brands #whomademyclothes?
